Word2Vec explainer — five steps from sliding window to embedding space and semantic arithmetic

The core idea — context defines meaning
Word2Vec is built on one hypothesis: words that appear in similar contexts have similar meanings. "Revolutionary" and "innovative" appear near the same neighbours — so they should end up near each other in vector space. The model never reads a dictionary. It learns purely from co-occurrence patterns.
The distributional hypothesis
"You shall know a word by the company it keeps." — J.R. Firth, 1957

Words appearing in similar sentence positions share semantic properties.
What Word2Vec actually does
Trains a shallow neural network to predict context words from a centre word. The learned weights become the embeddings — meaning is a side effect of the prediction task.
Word2Vec produces a dense vector (e.g. 300 numbers) for every word. Unlike one-hot or TF-IDF vectors, these are small, continuous, and capture semantic similarity — "king" and "queen" will be close; "king" and "broccoli" will be far apart.
Step 1 — the sliding window
A window of fixed size (typically 2–5 words) slides across every sentence. The centre word is the input; the surrounding context words are the prediction targets. Each position of the window generates one training example.
Sentence: "Our AI platform delivers revolutionary results" — window size = 2
Position 1 of 4
With a window of 2, each centre word generates up to 4 training pairs (2 left + 2 right). A corpus of 1 billion words generates hundreds of millions of training examples — this scale is what makes the embeddings rich.
Step 2 — the neural network (Skip-gram)
The Skip-gram architecture takes the centre word as input and tries to predict each context word. It has just one hidden layer — the weight matrix of this layer becomes the word embeddings.
The network is trained to predict context words from centre words. After training, we discard the output layer entirely. The rows of the hidden weight matrix W are the word embeddings — one 300-dimensional vector per word.
Step 3 — learning by backpropagation
For each training pair, the network makes a prediction, measures its error (how wrong was the predicted context word?), and nudges the embedding vectors slightly in the direction that reduces the error. After millions of examples, vectors for semantically similar words converge toward each other.
Training pair: centre="revolutionary", target="innovative"
Watch the loss shrink as the embedding for "revolutionary" gets nudged closer to "innovative"
The key insight: two words that repeatedly appear in the same context receive the same gradient signal — both get nudged in the same direction repeatedly. Over millions of examples this pressure accumulates, pulling semantically related words together in the vector space.
Step 4 — what the embeddings capture
After training on a large corpus, the embedding space develops rich geometric structure. Semantic similarity becomes spatial proximity. And remarkably, directions in the space encode relationships — the famous "king − man + woman = queen" arithmetic.
