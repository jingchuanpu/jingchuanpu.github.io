Words shared across clusters — bridging vectors
"AI" appears in hype docs (1, 4, 7) AND tech docs (3, 6, 9) → its vector sits between the two clusters
"platform" appears in hype docs alongside "revolutionary" AND alongside "model" → intermediate position
"performance" appears in both finance AND tech docs → bridging vector
This is why Word2Vec captures nuance that TF-IDF cannot. "AI" isn't simply a hype word or a tech word — its vector position reflects the fact that it appears in both contexts, placing it between those clusters in embedding space.

Vector arithmetic on our corpus embeddings
revolutionary−hype+precision=benchmark Remove hype framing, add rigour → gets technical
market−finance+training=benchmark Shift from finance domain to AI tech domain
AI+revenue=platform AI × finance context → product/platform framing
innovative−marketing+accuracy=precision Strip hype layer → substantive technical claim
Why this works geometrically
The direction from "revolutionary" to "benchmark" in our embedding space encodes the shift from marketing language to technical language — because those words appear in systematically different document types. Subtracting "hype" and adding "precision" follows the same direction, landing near "benchmark".
These analogies only work because our corpus has consistent structure: three clearly separated document types each with their own vocabulary. On a richer, more varied corpus the same geometric relationships emerge at finer granularity.

Sparse, high-dimensional. "revolutionary" and "innovative" are completely orthogonal — zero similarity. No notion of meaning or context.
TF-IDF

Still sparse. Still treats "revolutionary" and "innovative" as unrelated. Captures importance but not semantics.

Dense 300-dim vector. Cosine similarity with "innovative" = 0.92. Meaning encoded as geometry.
