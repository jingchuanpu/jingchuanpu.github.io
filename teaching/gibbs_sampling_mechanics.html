How Gibbs sampling works in LDA — a 6-step visual walkthrough from the core idea to convergence

The core idea — one variable at a time
Gibbs sampling breaks an intractable joint problem into a sequence of simple conditional ones. Instead of inferring all topic assignments at once, it holds everything fixed and updates just one word at a time.
The hard problem
Find the best topic assignment for all N words simultaneously.

With 90 words and 3 topics → 3⁹⁰ possible states.
Completely intractable.
The Gibbs trick
Fix all other assignments. Sample just one word's topic from its conditional distribution.

With everything else fixed → just 3 probabilities to compute.
Trivially easy.
The mathematical guarantee: if you repeat this process long enough — cycling through every variable repeatedly — the joint distribution of all variables converges to the true posterior. This is proven by Markov chain theory.
Step 1 — random initialization
Every word in every document gets a random topic assignment. This is the starting state — pure noise. The colors are meaningless at this point.

Every color is essentially random — "AI" landed in amber, "model" in purple, "revolutionary" in green. None of this reflects reality yet.
The starting state doesn't matter for the final result — only for how long it takes to converge. Gibbs sampling will find the same answer regardless of initialization, given enough iterations.
Step 2 — pick one word and remove its label
The sampler visits each word in sequence. For the current word, it temporarily removes its topic assignment — as if the word has no color yet. Everything else stays fixed.
Doc 1 — focusing on the word "revolutionary"

What we know about this doc
After removing "revolutionary": this document currently has 3 purple words, 4 green words, 2 amber words. So it leans slightly green.
What we know about "revolutionary"
Across all other documents, "revolutionary" appears 8× in amber, 1× in purple, 1× in green. So the corpus associates it strongly with amber.
By removing just one label at a time, all the counts we need are available from the current state of all other assignments. No expensive global computation required.
Step 3 — compute the conditional probability
Using the two signals (how does this document lean? how does the corpus use this word?), we compute a probability for each topic. The formula multiplies them together.
P(topic = k | word w, document d, everything else)
  ∝ (nwk + β) × (ndk + α)

nwk = times word w appears in topic k (across all docs)
ndk = times topic k appears in this document
α, β = smoothing priors (prevent zero probabilities)

Step 4 — draw a sample and re-assign
We don't just pick the highest probability — we sample from the distribution. Most of the time the highest-probability topic wins, but occasionally a lower-probability topic gets chosen. This randomness is essential: it lets the algorithm escape local traps and explore the full space.
Spin the wheel for "revolutionary":
AI tech 8% Finance 11% Hype 81%
A random draw lands at 74% → falls in the amber zone →
revolutionaryre-assigned to Topic C (hype) ✓
Why sample instead of just picking the max?
Pure greedy selection gets stuck. Sampling preserves exploration — a word can occasionally try a different topic, which can unlock better global configurations that greedy would never find.
What changes after re-assignment?
The counts update instantly. Now "revolutionary" contributes +1 to the amber word count and +1 to this document's amber count — making amber slightly more attractive for the next word.
This is a Markov chain: the next state depends only on the current state. After enough steps, the chain "mixes" and the samples it produces are effectively draws from the true posterior distribution.
Step 5 — sweep, repeat, converge
One full iteration sweeps through every word in every document, re-sampling each one. After hundreds of sweeps, assignments stop changing — topics have crystallized.

Convergence is detected when the number of re-assignments per iteration drops to zero — or practically, when it stays below a small threshold for several consecutive sweeps. The final topic–word counts are then read off as the model output.
